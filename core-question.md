## The central question I am investigating

> *How could AI-driven automation create systemic risk in African economies through labour market disruption — and what governance interventions could reduce this risk?*

---

## Why I care about this (write this yourself)

For the past six years, I have worked with a programme in Kenya focused on digital skills training for job creation. In that time, I have watched one pattern repeat: a job guaranteed today is gone tomorrow. Platforms that once had consistent work to distribute now sit empty. Freelancers invest their time, energy, and money into skills that become obsolete before they can earn back what they spent. Scams spread in the vacuum left by the collapse of trusted work platforms.

This has been my lived pain point — not abstract concern, but something I have watched happen to real people trying to build a livelihood through digital work.

And now, with AI automation accelerating, those fears have sharpened into something more urgent. The World Bank's 2026 report on AI and job displacement confirms what I was already seeing on the ground: displacement is no longer a future concern — it is already happening, and it could be a matter of time before it affects everyone. That is why I care, and why I need to do something about it.

---

## What I think I know (starting assumptions)

These are my current beliefs — they may be wrong, and that's okay. The point is to write them down so I can test them.

1. AI is already displacing some categories of work that African economies depend on — particularly in BPO, data annotation, and routine services.

2. Africa's labour markets are different from Western ones in ways that matter: higher informality, younger populations, weaker social safety nets, and faster demographic growth. This means the same level of displacement could have more severe consequences.

3. Current AI governance discussions largely happen in the US and EU — they are not designed with African labour markets in mind.

4. If job displacement accelerates without governance responses, the downstream effects could include: increased youth unemployment → political instability → weakened state capacity → reduced ability to govern AI at all. This is a vicious cycle.

5. I am not sure yet whether this qualifies as a "global catastrophic risk" in the technical AI safety sense, or something different. That is one of the things I need to figure out.

## How this connects to AI safety

Systemic risks, according to the AI Safety Atlas, are ones that integrate AI with complex global systems, creating emergent threats — such as mass unemployment from automation, power concentration where AI becomes monopolised, and AI-generated content flooding information systems and causing epistemic erosion.

These risks can be broken down by scale: individual or local risks affecting thousands to hundreds of thousands; catastrophic risks threatening 10% of the global population, more widespread geographically — mass unemployment that destabilises economies, creates social unrest, and produces political upheaval; and existential risks, where AI threatens something humanity could not recover from.

What connects my work to AI safety is this: the pathway from mass unemployment to governance collapse is not just an economic problem — it is a risk amplifier. When societies are destabilised by displacement, their capacity to regulate AI weakens at exactly the moment that capacity is needed most. This means governance failure is not a side issue — it is a core mechanism by which AI harms escalate.

The specific actors who need to act are African governments (early warning systems for labour market disruption), international bodies (frameworks requiring tech companies to disclose displacement impacts), and civil society (building the research base that makes good policy possible). Without deliberate intervention at these levels — before we reach catastrophic scale — the window to govern AI closes.
